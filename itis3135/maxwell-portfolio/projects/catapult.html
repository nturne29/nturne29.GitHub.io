Mangonel Catapult Project

Dynamics Class Group Project | Spring 2023

Designing a Mangonel Catapult for Accuracy and Range

As project lead for a team of four in UNC Charlotte’s Dynamics course, I guided the design and construction of a mangonel-style catapult to achieve maximum accuracy and range. With Dylan DiQuarto, Simon Adams, and Hailey Williams, we selected a mangonel for its stability and power, achieving a launch distance of 122.5 ft—within 2% of our calculated 125.2 ft.

Our iterative process, from calculations to prototyping, led to a first-place finish among 20+ teams, overcoming initial failures through innovative design improvements.

Initial Calculations

Using dynamics principles, I performed hand calculations to predict the catapult’s performance, estimating a launch velocity of 66.8 ft/s for a tennis ball. Kinematic analysis and moment of inertia for the lacrosse stick arm yielded a projected distance of 125.2 ft. The actual distance of 122.5 ft was within 2%, validating our analytical approach.

Design & Prototyping

The mangonel design was chosen for its stability, power, and cost-effectiveness, as determined by a decision matrix evaluating frame strength, stability, ease of construction, cost, and power. We used repurposed 2x4 and 4x4 lumber, a lacrosse stick arm, and a polyethylene rope torsion engine. The initial prototype failed due to shear stress, prompting a redesign with truss supports.

Final Prototype: Learning from Mistakes

The final prototype addressed shear stress with 45° truss supports, 90° tie plates, and a thicker 3/8" polyethylene rope (40 ft, 5 wraps). The lacrosse stick arm struck a 26-inch hurdle at a 73° departure angle for consistency. A winding mechanism with 2-inch pipe handles and a safe-launch paracord trigger enhanced performance and safety.

In-Depth Testing & Data Collection

Testing in a parking lot showed the first prototype (1/4" rope, 25 ft, 3 wraps) losing tension, with distances up to 73 ft. The second prototype (3/8" rope, 40 ft, 5 wraps) reached 110 ft at 6 twists. On test day, 7 twists achieved 122.5 ft. The competition results and test launch video are below.

Technical Breakdown

The mangonel’s key components ensured accuracy and range, as detailed below.

Component Description
Frame 2x4 and 4x4 lumber with 45° trusses and tie plates for shear resistance.
Torsion Engine 3/8" polyethylene rope, 40 ft, 5 wraps, with 2-inch pipe handles.
Lacrosse Stick 30-inch arm, 28-inch exposed, for consistent projectile launch.
Hurdle 26-inch height, 73° departure angle for optimal trajectory.
Winding Mechanism 2-inch pipe handles with rod insert for torque multiplication.
Safe-Launch System Paracord trigger for remote firing from 15 feet.
