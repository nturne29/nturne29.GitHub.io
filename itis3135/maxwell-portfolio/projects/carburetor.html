Reverse Engineering a Briggs and Stratton 725EX Carburetor

For my CAD II final project, I chose to reverse engineer a float-style carburetor from a Briggs and Stratton 725EX lawnmower. This component is critical for mixing air and fuel for combustion in small engines, valued for its mechanical simplicity and reliability without computer programming.

Modeling this part in SolidWorks challenged my skills due to its complex geometry, requiring precise measurements with digital calipers and creative problem-solving to capture intricate features like the venturi and metering pin.

Project Overview

The carburetor regulates the air-to-fuel ratio for efficient engine performance, using mechanical components like a float, metering pin, and butterfly valves. I purchased a third-party replica for $10 to disassemble and measure, sketching complex components on grid paper to visualize hidden features. This hands-on approach, combined with SolidWorks, allowed me to create an accurate 3D model.

My goal was to test my SolidWorks proficiency by modeling a part with challenging edges, chamfers, and small-scale features. Using techniques like the shell command and taper pins, I translated physical measurements into a detailed digital model, overcoming limitations in measuring tiny components with innovative methods, such as using pencil lead to gauge depths.

Technical Breakdown

The carburetor consists of several key components, each with a specific role in fuel delivery. Below is a table summarizing the major parts, adapted from my project writeup, along with a gallery showcasing the modeling process.

Part # Part Name Description
109 Major Valve Stem (Offset) Actuates the major butterfly valve to allow air & fuel mix to pass to combustion.
97 Minor Valve Stem (Throttle) Direct connection to throttle pedal to actuate intake butterfly valve.
108 Major Butterfly Valve Controls flow of air/fuel to the combustion chamber.
130 Minor Butterfly Valve (Throttle) Smaller valve that primarily allows air through the carburetor venturi.
134 Metering Pin Controlled by the float, allows more/less fuel to travel to the atomization chamber.
133 Fuel Bowl Float Dependent on fuel level, actuates metering pin for fuel delivery control.
104 Float Pin Retains fuel float to the body of the carburetor, so it remains fixed.
137 Fuel Bowl Gasket Allows the fuel bowl to retain fuel safely with a watertight seal.
276 Banjo Bolt Gasket Allows the banjo bolt to have a seal with the base of the fuel bowl.
117 Banjo Bolt Used for transferring excess pressurized fuel safely through the intake stalk.
125 Carburetor Body Where air and fuel are mixed, and then delivered to the combustion chamber(s).
975 Fuel Bowl Intermediate fuel storage as gasoline moves through the system.
